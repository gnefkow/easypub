## Who did typesetting for the Gutenberg press?

- **Gutenberg didn’t “typeset” like modern digital layout**—but he (and his workshop) **set metal type by hand** into forms for each page.
- Historically, it was very much a **team workshop effort**: compositors, pressmen, and others. Gutenberg is credited with the overall invention/engineering and production, but **not every page would have been personally composed by him**.
- For the Gutenberg Bible specifically, Gutenberg worked with business partners/financiers (famously **Johann Fust**) and a key craftsman (**Peter Schöffer**, often described as a skilled scribe-turned-printer and later a major printer himself). Schöffer is one of the strongest “early printing/typesetting” name inspirations.

If you like the “name it after a person” route, a few strong contenders are **Aldus**, **Tschichold**, **Baskerville**, **Garamond**, or **Schöffer** (very Gutenberg-era).

## Graphic/print people with real anarchist-pamphlet energy (late 1800s–early 1900s)

If you want **“libre information”** vibes rooted in *actual* anarchist print culture (not “chaos”), the richest seam is the French-language ecosystem around **Jean Grave’s** journal **`Les Temps nouveaux`** (1895–1914). It deliberately fused **political text + page design + prints/posters**.

- **Maximilien Luce**: made at least one prominent promotional poster for *Les Temps nouveaux* (very direct “movement print” aesthetic).
- **Paul Signac**: openly anarchist-aligned; contributed imagery and materially supported anarchist publishing.
- **Camille Pissarro** and **Lucien Pissarro**: contributors within that circle; strong “art meets radical print” lineage.

## Who did the typesetting/printing for Proudhon and Bakunin?

For **Proudhon** and **Bakunin**, “who typeset it?” is usually less a single famous name and more a **network of working printers/typographers** plus whatever shop would risk seizures.

- **Reality of the time**: radical newspapers/pamphlets were often **handset type** in commercial shops (sometimes sympathetic, sometimes purely transactional), and the imprint/printer line could **change frequently** due to raids, bans, money, or logistics.
- **Why it’s hard to attribute**: many editions were **seized**, moved, or printed under shifting arrangements; the “production credit” is often missing, inconsistent, or not the part historians foreground.
- **A concrete example of identifiable “production houses”** (from the anarchist press itself): *Les Temps nouveaux* was printed by **Imprimerie Charles Blot** (Paris) until 1903, then **Imprimerie G. Chaponet** (same address). Those are the kinds of “shops behind the movement” you can safely name-check.

So: yes, Gutenberg had a workshop; similarly, Proudhon/Bakunin had **print ecosystems**, not a single celebrated typesetter.
