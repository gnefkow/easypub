# 5.3 Internationalization and Global Considerations

If you have only worked with English-language books, it is easy to assume that text flows left to right, lines stack top to bottom, and letters are self-explanatory units that do not need pronunciation guides. Those assumptions are specific to a small fraction of the world's writing systems. When you work with Arabic, Hebrew, Japanese, Chinese, Korean, or dozens of other languages, the rules change: text direction, line stacking, character encoding, and even the relationship between base characters and annotations become explicit concerns.

EPUB is built on web standards, and those standards have spent decades addressing internationalization. The good news is that the mechanisms exist: you can declare text direction, set vertical writing modes, embed any Unicode character, annotate pronunciation, and signal language for accessibility tools. The challenge is knowing when and how to use these mechanisms so your EPUB renders correctly across reading systems that vary in their support.

This chapter introduces the core internationalization concepts you need to handle global content in EPUB. The goal is not to make you an expert in every script, but to give you a mental model for what changes when you move beyond left-to-right Latin text, and to show you the specific attributes, CSS properties, and markup patterns that make international EPUBs work.

## Key vocabulary (for this chapter)

**Internationalization** (often abbreviated **i18n**) is the process of designing content and software so it can be adapted for different languages, scripts, and regions. **Right-to-left (RTL)** describes scripts like Arabic and Hebrew where text flows from right to left within a line. **Left-to-right (LTR)** is the default direction for Latin, Cyrillic, Greek, and many other scripts. **Writing mode** refers to the direction in which lines of text are stacked; horizontal writing modes stack lines vertically (top to bottom or bottom to top), while **vertical writing modes** stack lines horizontally (right to left or left to right), as used in traditional Chinese, Japanese, and Korean (CJK) typography. The **`dir` attribute** in HTML/XHTML declares the base text direction of an element (`ltr` or `rtl`). The **`writing-mode` CSS property** controls whether text runs horizontally or vertically. **Unicode** is the universal character encoding standard that assigns a unique code point to virtually every character in every writing system. **UTF-8** is the most common encoding of Unicode and is required for EPUB content documents. The **`xml:lang` attribute** declares the language of content, which affects rendering (hyphenation, font selection) and accessibility (screen reader pronunciation). **Ruby annotations** are small characters placed above or beside base text to indicate pronunciation or meaning, commonly used in Japanese (furigana) and Chinese (pinyin or bopomofo).

## Why internationalization matters for EPUB authors

Even if you are authoring content in a single language, understanding internationalization helps you produce cleaner, more robust EPUBs. Language declarations affect hyphenation algorithms, screen reader behavior, and search indexing. Character encoding mistakes can turn readable text into garbled symbols. And if you ever quote, cite, or include content from another language, knowing how to mark it up correctly prevents rendering surprises.

If you are producing multilingual content, or content in a non-Latin script, internationalization moves from "nice to have" to "essential." A book in Arabic that does not declare RTL direction will be unreadable. A Japanese novel with vertical layout that does not set the correct writing mode will feel foreign to its intended audience. And CJK text without ruby annotations may be inaccessible to readers who need pronunciation guidance.

The EPUB spec inherits internationalization support from HTML, CSS, and XML. That inheritance is powerful, but it also means you need to understand which attributes and properties to use, and where to declare them so reading systems interpret your intent correctly.

## Text direction: RTL scripts and the `dir` attribute

The most fundamental internationalization concern is text direction. English, French, Russian, and most European languages read left to right. Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, and Urdu read right to left. When you mix these scripts, or when you author primarily in an RTL language, you need to tell the reading system which direction applies.

In XHTML, you declare text direction with the `dir` attribute. The two values are `ltr` (left to right) and `rtl` (right to left). You can set this attribute on any element, but the most important place is the root `<html>` element of each content document:

```xml
<html xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" xml:lang="ar" dir="rtl">
```

This tells the reading system that the default text direction for the document is right to left. Paragraphs will align to the right, punctuation will appear on the correct side, and the logical flow of content will match reader expectations.

When you have mixed-direction content (for example, an Arabic paragraph that includes an English title), you can override direction locally:

```xml
<p>الكتاب بعنوان <span dir="ltr">"The Great Gatsby"</span> مشهور جداً.</p>
```

The `<span>` with `dir="ltr"` tells the rendering engine to treat the English text as a left-to-right segment embedded in the surrounding right-to-left context. Without this, punctuation and word order can break in confusing ways.

The Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm (often called "bidi") handles much of the complexity automatically, but it relies on correct `dir` declarations to know the overall context. If you omit `dir` on an RTL document, reading systems may guess incorrectly, and your text will render backward or with misplaced punctuation.

## CSS and the `writing-mode` property

Beyond left-right direction, some scripts traditionally stack lines in a different orientation. Japanese, Chinese, and Korean can be written vertically, with lines running from top to bottom and columns progressing from right to left. This is common in literary fiction, poetry, and traditional publishing in East Asia.

CSS provides the `writing-mode` property to control this. The most relevant values for EPUB are:

- `horizontal-tb`: horizontal text, lines stacked top to bottom (the default for most Western content)
- `vertical-rl`: vertical text, lines stacked right to left (traditional CJK vertical layout)
- `vertical-lr`: vertical text, lines stacked left to right (less common, used in some Mongolian contexts)

To set vertical layout for an entire content document, you might write:

```css
html {
  writing-mode: vertical-rl;
}
```

Or you can scope it to specific elements if only part of the book uses vertical layout:

```css
.vertical-chapter {
  writing-mode: vertical-rl;
}
```

When `writing-mode` is vertical, the meaning of "width" and "height" effectively rotates. Margins, padding, and text alignment behave relative to the new orientation. This can be confusing at first, but the CSS specification defines how properties map in each writing mode.

Reading system support for vertical writing modes has improved significantly, especially in readers targeting East Asian markets. However, support is not universal. If your audience includes readers on older devices or less specialized reading apps, you should test carefully and consider whether a horizontal fallback is acceptable.

## Combining direction and writing mode

For most content, you use either `dir` (for RTL horizontal text) or `writing-mode` (for vertical CJK text), but not both at once. However, the two concepts are orthogonal: `dir` controls the inline direction (which way characters flow within a line), while `writing-mode` controls the block direction (which way lines stack).

In vertical Japanese, text flows top to bottom within a line, and lines stack right to left. The inline direction is "top to bottom," and the block direction is "right to left." You do not need `dir="rtl"` for this; vertical-rl handles it. The `dir` attribute is specifically about the left-right distinction within horizontal text or the logical "start" and "end" of inline content.

If you are working with a script that is both vertical and requires RTL inline direction (a rare combination), the CSS logical properties and values spec provides tools, but in practice most EPUB internationalization falls into one of two buckets: horizontal RTL (Arabic, Hebrew) or vertical CJK.

## Unicode and UTF-8: the only encoding you need

EPUB requires that content documents be encoded in UTF-8. This is not optional, and it simplifies your life: you do not need to choose between encodings or worry about legacy character sets.

UTF-8 is a variable-length encoding of Unicode. Unicode assigns a unique code point to virtually every character in every writing system, from Latin letters to Chinese ideographs to emoji. UTF-8 can represent any Unicode code point, and it is backward-compatible with ASCII for the basic Latin characters.

In your XHTML content documents, you declare the encoding in the XML declaration at the top of the file:

```xml
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?>
```

Most tools generate this automatically, and most reading systems assume UTF-8 even if the declaration is missing. But including it explicitly is good practice, and some validators will warn if it is absent.

The practical implication is that you can include Arabic, Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Cyrillic, Greek, Hebrew, Thai, Devanagari, or any other script directly in your XHTML files. You do not need to use HTML entities or escape sequences for non-ASCII characters. Just make sure your text editor is saving the file as UTF-8 (most modern editors do this by default).

If you ever see garbled text (often called "mojibake") when opening an EPUB, the most common cause is an encoding mismatch: the file was saved in one encoding but interpreted as another. Sticking to UTF-8 everywhere and verifying your editor settings eliminates this class of problem.

## Language declarations: the `xml:lang` attribute

Declaring the language of your content is one of the simplest internationalization steps, and one of the most impactful. The `xml:lang` attribute tells reading systems, accessibility tools, and search engines what language the text is in.

You should declare the primary language on the root `<html>` element of each content document:

```xml
<html xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" xml:lang="ja">
```

This example declares Japanese as the document language. For a French book, you would use `xml:lang="fr"`. For American English, `xml:lang="en-US"`. The values are BCP 47 language tags, which combine a language code (like `en`, `fr`, `ja`, `ar`) with optional region and script subtags.

Language declarations matter for several reasons:

First, they affect **hyphenation**. Reading systems that support automatic hyphenation use language-specific rules. English hyphenation is different from German hyphenation, and both are different from French. If you declare the wrong language, hyphenation can produce awkward or incorrect breaks.

Second, they affect **screen reader pronunciation**. A screen reader encountering Japanese text will switch to a Japanese voice if one is available, but only if the content is marked as Japanese. Without language declarations, the screen reader may try to pronounce Japanese characters as if they were a different language, producing nonsense.

Third, they affect **font selection** in some reading systems. CJK characters can have different standard forms in Chinese, Japanese, and Korean (the same code point may render with slightly different strokes). A reading system that knows the language can choose the appropriate font variant.

If your book contains passages in a different language, you can override the language locally:

```xml
<p xml:lang="en">This is an English paragraph in an otherwise Japanese book.</p>
```

Or at an even finer level:

```xml
<p>日本語の文章に <span xml:lang="en">English words</span> が含まれています。</p>
```

The key habit is to always declare the primary language at the document level and to mark up language switches when they occur.

## Ruby annotations for East Asian text

Ruby is a typographic convention where small characters appear above or beside base text to provide pronunciation or meaning. In Japanese, this is called furigana: small hiragana characters above kanji to show how the kanji should be read. In Chinese, ruby can show pinyin (romanization) or bopomofo (zhuyin) above characters.

HTML5 and EPUB 3 support ruby through dedicated elements: `<ruby>`, `<rt>`, and optionally `<rp>`.

Here is a simple example in Japanese, where the kanji 漢字 is annotated with its reading かんじ:

```xml
<ruby>漢字<rt>かんじ</rt></ruby>
```

The `<ruby>` element wraps the base text and its annotation. The `<rt>` (ruby text) element contains the annotation. Reading systems that support ruby will display かんじ above 漢字 in a smaller font.

For reading systems that do not support ruby (or for plain-text fallback), you can include `<rp>` (ruby parenthesis) elements to provide fallback punctuation:

```xml
<ruby>漢字<rp>(</rp><rt>かんじ</rt><rp>)</rp></ruby>
```

In a supporting reader, the parentheses are hidden and the ruby displays normally. In a non-supporting reader, the output might appear as 漢字(かんじ), which is less elegant but still readable.

Ruby support varies across reading systems. Most modern readers targeting Japanese or Chinese markets handle ruby well. Readers without CJK focus may render ruby awkwardly or fall back to the parenthetical display. If ruby is central to your book (for example, a children's book with furigana on every kanji), you should test on representative devices to ensure readability.

CSS also provides properties for fine-tuning ruby appearance (`ruby-position`, `ruby-align`), but support is inconsistent. For most use cases, the default rendering is acceptable, and you should avoid complex ruby styling unless you have tested it thoroughly.

## Practical workflow: authoring international EPUBs

If you are creating an EPUB in a non-Latin script or with significant internationalization concerns, here is a practical workflow:

Start by setting the correct language and direction on every content document. This means `xml:lang` on the `<html>` element, and `dir="rtl"` if the primary script is right to left. If you use vertical layout, set `writing-mode` in your CSS.

Make sure your source files are saved as UTF-8. Check your text editor settings, and if you are converting from another format (Word, InDesign, legacy HTML), verify that the conversion preserved encoding correctly.

Use ruby markup for any text that requires pronunciation annotations. Keep the markup simple and test on at least one reader that supports ruby.

Declare language switches inline when your content mixes languages. This is especially important if you quote foreign-language sources or include glossaries.

Validate with EPUBCheck. EPUBCheck will catch some internationalization issues (like missing language declarations in the OPF metadata), though it will not catch every rendering problem. After validation, test on representative reading systems. For RTL content, test on readers that your Arabic or Hebrew audience actually uses. For vertical CJK, test on readers popular in Japan, China, or Korea.

Finally, remember that user settings can interact with internationalization. Some readers allow users to override fonts, and a user-selected font may not include the glyphs you need. Embedded fonts with appropriate Unicode coverage can help, but you should also ensure your content degrades gracefully if fonts are overridden.

## Common pitfalls and gotchas

The first pitfall is forgetting to declare `dir="rtl"` on RTL documents. Without this declaration, Arabic and Hebrew text may render with incorrect alignment, punctuation on the wrong side, or confusing word order. This is one of the most common causes of "the book looks broken" reports for RTL content.

The second pitfall is assuming that `writing-mode: vertical-rl` is universally supported. Support has improved, but older readers or readers outside the CJK market may not handle vertical text well. Test before committing to vertical layout for your entire book, and consider whether a horizontal fallback is acceptable for some audiences.

The third pitfall is encoding confusion. If your file is saved in a legacy encoding (like Shift_JIS or ISO-8859-1) but declared as UTF-8, or vice versa, text will display as garbage. Modern tools default to UTF-8, but if you inherit content from older systems, verify encoding before importing.

The fourth pitfall is omitting `xml:lang` declarations. This affects hyphenation, screen reader behavior, and sometimes font selection. It is easy to forget, especially if you are focused on visual appearance rather than semantics. Treat language declarations as a required part of your XHTML boilerplate.

The fifth pitfall is complex ruby styling. The basic `<ruby>` and `<rt>` elements work reasonably well across supporting readers. Advanced CSS ruby properties are less consistently supported. If your ruby looks correct in one reader but wrong in another, simplify your styling before debugging further.

The sixth pitfall is mixing direction and writing mode incorrectly. For RTL horizontal text, use `dir="rtl"`. For vertical CJK, use `writing-mode: vertical-rl`. Combining them without understanding the interaction can produce unexpected layouts. In most cases, you need one or the other, not both.

The seventh pitfall is testing only on readers that share your own locale. If your book targets Arabic readers, test on readers popular in Arabic-speaking regions. If it targets Japanese readers, test on Japanese-market readers. Readers designed for different markets often have different levels of internationalization support, and testing on a Western-focused reader will not catch all the issues your actual audience will encounter.

When you treat internationalization as a set of explicit declarations (language, direction, encoding, annotations) rather than implicit assumptions, your EPUBs become portable across the world's scripts and reading systems. That portability is one of EPUB's strengths as a global format, but it only works when authors use the mechanisms the spec provides.
