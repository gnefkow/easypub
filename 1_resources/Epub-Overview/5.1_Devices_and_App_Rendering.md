# 5.1 Devices and App Rendering

If you have only tested your EPUB in one app, it is easy to assume that an EPUB “looks the way it looks.” Then you open the same file on a different device and everything changes: the font is different, margins are different, page breaks fall in awkward places, a drop cap disappears, or an SVG that was crisp becomes a blurry raster image. If you come from the web, this might feel like the bad old days of cross-browser testing—but worse, because you cannot simply tell the user to “update their browser,” and because many reading systems intentionally override your design for accessibility and personal preference.

This chapter is about building a mental model of why those differences happen. Once you understand what a reading system is doing (and what it is allowed to do), rendering variation stops feeling random. You also learn how to test in a way that catches the big differences without turning your life into an endless device lab.

## Key vocabulary (for this chapter)

A **reading system** is the software that opens and displays an EPUB (an app like Apple Books, a device UI like a Kobo e-reader, or desktop software like Thorium). A **rendering engine** is the HTML/CSS layout engine inside the reading system that parses XHTML and applies CSS (conceptually similar to “a browser engine,” even if it is embedded in an app). **Pagination** is the process of turning reflowable content into “pages” based on screen size, font size, line spacing, and reader settings; it is not the same thing as a web page scrolling in a browser. **User overrides** are the settings and injected styles a reader applies on top of the publisher’s CSS (font choice, font size, margins, line height, theme/night mode, etc.). **E-ink** is a display technology used by many dedicated e-readers; it has very different performance and color characteristics than LCD/OLED screens. **Sideloading** is loading an EPUB onto a device outside of a store’s official purchase/download path. **Readium** is a widely used open-source EPUB rendering toolkit that powers several apps and platforms. **KF8/KFX** are Kindle ecosystem formats; in many Kindle workflows your EPUB is converted into a Kindle-specific format before a user reads it.

## The landscape of EPUB reading: it is not one platform

When you ship a web page, you are implicitly shipping to a small number of browser engines that behave roughly the same way: Chromium-based browsers, Safari/WebKit, and Firefox. EPUB looks similar at first because it uses XHTML and CSS, but the platform reality is more fragmented.

Some people read on dedicated e-ink devices that prioritize battery life and readability. Some read on phones where the reading system is one app among many, operating inside a constrained mobile environment. Some read on tablets with powerful rendering engines and rich typography features. Others read on desktops using apps designed for libraries, accessibility, or publishing workflows. And on top of that, there is the store and DRM layer: the file the user ends up reading may not be byte-for-byte identical to what you exported if it was processed or converted along the way.

This variety is why EPUB authoring best practices emphasize conservative HTML/CSS, clear structure, and robust fallbacks. You are not styling “one screen.” You are describing a publication that will be reinterpreted across different hardware, software, and user preferences.

## A practical mental model: three layers control what the reader sees

When your EPUB is displayed, you can think of three layers contributing to the final result.

The first layer is **your content and CSS**: the XHTML documents in the spine, linked stylesheets, embedded fonts, and images you shipped. This is the part you control directly.

The second layer is the **reading system’s rules and engine**. The reading system decides how to paginate, what CSS features it supports, whether it allows JavaScript, how it handles internal links, what it does with the navigation document, and how it applies default styles. Even if two reading systems use similar underlying engine technology, their “EPUB wrapper” behaviors can differ (for example, one might preserve more publisher styling, while another aggressively normalizes typography).

The third layer is **user overrides**. Many reading systems treat your EPUB like a document the reader owns: the reader can pick font size, font family, line spacing, margins, and themes. Some overrides are explicit settings the user chooses; others are implicit (for example, a reading system’s default stylesheet). This is not a bug. It is part of why EPUB is popular: readers can adapt a book to their eyes, their lighting, and their needs.

As a result, EPUB design is less about pixel-perfect layout and more about resilient typography and structure. If you are aiming for exact layout fidelity, that is usually a sign you want a fixed-layout EPUB (Chapter 3.6), not a reflowable one.

## Dedicated e-ink devices: why they behave differently

E-ink devices are designed around two priorities: long battery life and a paper-like reading experience. That hardware reality shapes how the software behaves.

E-ink screens have slower refresh and often limited color. This can influence animation support, video/audio expectations, and sometimes the responsiveness of complex layouts. A design that feels fine on a tablet can feel heavy on an e-ink device, especially if it uses large images, heavy embedded fonts, or CSS patterns that trigger expensive reflow.

E-ink reading also tends to encourage strong user control over typography. Readers frequently adjust font size, margins, and line spacing. This means that any design that depends on a specific line length, a specific number of lines per page, or a precise relationship between text and images is likely to break. In reflowable EPUBs, that is normal: page breaks are a function of user preference as much as author intent.

For practical authoring, the e-ink lesson is to optimize for clarity and structural correctness. Make headings real headings. Make lists real lists. Make footnote links robust. Use images that scale and do not require a particular viewport. Avoid “layout hacks” that only hold together at one font size.

## Software readers: the same EPUB can be interpreted differently

Many readers will encounter your EPUB through software on phones, tablets, or desktop computers. The names in the ecosystem matter because they tend to represent different behaviors and different constraints.

Apple Books is a major consumer reader on Apple platforms. Google Play Books is a major consumer reader on Android and the web. Adobe Digital Editions (often shortened to ADE) is common in library and publisher workflows and historically represents a stricter, more “spec-ish” world in some areas, while being idiosyncratic in others. Thorium Reader is a popular desktop reading system built on modern web technology and Readium. Calibre’s viewer is widely used by authors and publishers for inspection and conversion workflows.

What matters for you is not memorizing brand names, but understanding that different reading systems can make different choices in at least five areas:

First, **CSS support** differs. Some reading systems support a modern subset of CSS, while others lag behind or intentionally ignore properties that conflict with user settings. The same stylesheet can produce different margins, hyphenation, or font behavior depending on what is supported and what is overridden.

Second, **font handling** differs. Embedded fonts might be honored, partially honored, or ignored. Some reading systems let the user override the publisher’s font choice entirely. Others allow embedded fonts only when the user explicitly permits them. If typography is critical, you need to test and you need reasonable fallbacks.

Third, **pagination and widows/orphans behavior** differs. Even when the text is the same, the algorithm that decides page breaks is not standardized to the level that web layout in a scrolling page is. If a page break is “ugly,” it may be unavoidable in a particular reading system at a particular font size.

Fourth, **navigation UI** differs. Even if your nav document is valid, one reading system may show a hierarchical table of contents, another may flatten it, another may prioritize landmarks, and another may show only spine order. Your job is to provide good navigation metadata; the reading system’s job is to present it in a way that fits its UX.

Fifth, **scripting and interactive features** differ dramatically. EPUB 3 allows more than older EPUB versions, but the practical support landscape is conservative. Many reading systems disable JavaScript for security and battery reasons. If you rely on scripting for core reading functionality, your EPUB will fail for many readers. The realistic approach is progressive enhancement: the book must work without scripts, and scripts (if used at all) should only enhance.

## Rendering engines: EPUB uses web tech, but it is not “the web”

It is useful to know that most EPUB reading systems are built on some form of HTML/CSS rendering engine. That can tempt you into thinking you can preview your EPUB in a browser and call it done. A browser preview can be helpful for debugging markup and basic CSS, but it is not a perfect simulation for three reasons.

First, reading systems apply **their own default styles** and may inject CSS for pagination, selection, highlights, and themes. A browser is not paginating your content the way an EPUB reader does. When you see different results, you are often seeing different defaults rather than “broken CSS.”

Second, reading systems have **policy decisions** that a browser does not. A reading system may clamp margins, change font sizes, or ignore certain CSS to preserve readability and accessibility. It may also choose to map certain semantic structures into its own UX features (for example, treating `epub:type="noteref"` links specially).

Third, the EPUB context has **constraints** that are not present on the open web. There is no network access for remote resources in many contexts. There are security restrictions around scripting. File URLs and relative paths resolve inside a container. And media type declarations and manifest rules matter in a way that a browser file-open does not replicate.

In other words, “it renders in Chrome” is a useful debugging clue, but it is not a compatibility guarantee.

## A concrete example: CSS that is valid but not respected

Consider a common desire: you want the publisher’s font to be used for body text. You embed a font and declare it in CSS:

```css
@font-face {
  font-family: "MyBookFont";
  src: url("../fonts/MyBookFont-Regular.woff2");
}

body {
  font-family: "MyBookFont", serif;
}
```

This is reasonable CSS. But in a reading system, several things can happen. The reader might not support `woff2` and only support `otf` or `ttf`. The reading system might support the font but still let the user override the font choice, so the user’s preferred font wins. Or the reading system might require a setting like “publisher fonts” to be enabled before it honors embedded fonts.

The takeaway is not “embedded fonts are pointless.” The takeaway is that publisher CSS is not the only authority. Your job is to supply a correct and accessible default, plus graceful fallbacks, and then test how those defaults behave in the readers that matter to your audience.

## The Kindle ecosystem: why “EPUB support” often means “conversion”

Amazon’s Kindle ecosystem is important enough that it deserves special treatment in your mental model. In many Kindle workflows, the device is not reading your EPUB directly. Instead, your EPUB is ingested and converted into a Kindle format (historically KF8, more recently KFX in many contexts). This is true whether you publish through a store pipeline or use a “send to device” workflow that accepts EPUB as input.

What this means in practice is that there are two compatibility questions, not one. The first question is whether your EPUB is clean and spec-conformant enough to ingest and convert reliably. The second question is how the converted output behaves on Kindle devices and apps. A book can be a perfectly valid EPUB and still look different after conversion, because the conversion process is not a pure identity transform. It maps features, sometimes drops features, and sometimes “approximates” CSS.

If Kindle is a target, you should treat Kindle testing as testing a converted artifact. Use tools that preview the Kindle output and be conservative about relying on advanced layout features that are known to be fragile across conversions.

## Testing strategy: be systematic without testing everything

Once you accept that “rendering varies,” the next question is how to test efficiently. The goal is not exhaustive device coverage. The goal is to catch the major engine differences and the major UX differences that affect real readers.

A useful approach is to pick a small set of reading systems that represent different “families” and then test the parts of the book that are most likely to break. In practical terms, you want at least one modern Readium-based reader (because Readium represents a common baseline and tends to be reasonably spec-aware), at least one major consumer app in your target ecosystem, and at least one strict or legacy-leaning reader if your distribution channels include libraries or enterprise workflows.

What do you test? Start with the structural and navigation features that affect whether the book is usable at all. Confirm the table of contents loads and matches the spine. Confirm internal links work. Confirm footnotes jump and return. Confirm the cover appears in the library view and in the book where appropriate. Then test typography and layout under user overrides: increase font size, change font family, change margins, switch to night mode. If the book remains readable and coherent under these changes, it is likely to behave well for real users.

Finally, test “stress” content. Pages with wide images, complex tables (if you have them), code blocks, poetry, and multi-level lists are the places where engines diverge. If you only test a chapter of plain paragraphs, you will miss the real problems.

This mindset aligns with Chapter 4.2 and 4.3: validate to ensure the structure is sound, package cleanly, then test in representative reading systems to see how the ecosystem interprets your valid EPUB.

## Common pitfalls and gotchas

The first gotcha is treating a reading system like a browser and expecting browser-like consistency. EPUB readers paginate, apply their own defaults, and intentionally allow user overrides. If you are chasing pixel-perfect fidelity in a reflowable EPUB, you are fighting the format.

The second gotcha is assuming that “publisher CSS wins.” Many readers prioritize readability and accessibility, and they may ignore or clamp CSS that conflicts with user settings. Your job is to provide a strong default experience that remains readable when overridden, not to enforce a single typographic outcome.

The third gotcha is using advanced CSS because it works in one modern desktop reader and then being surprised when it fails elsewhere. A feature can be valid CSS and still be unsupported or partially supported in a reading system. When in doubt, prefer simpler layout patterns and test early.

The fourth gotcha is not testing user controls. Readers will change font size, line spacing, and margins. They will switch themes. They will rotate their phone. If your book only looks acceptable at one default setting, it will not feel robust in the real world.

The fifth gotcha is misunderstanding Kindle behavior. If you deliver EPUB into a pipeline that converts it, you must test the converted output. Problems that appear “only on Kindle” are often conversion artifacts rather than EPUB validity issues.

The sixth gotcha is over-indexing on one tool as “the truth.” A validator can tell you structural correctness, but it cannot tell you how a particular app will choose to override fonts. A desktop reader might be a good baseline, but it might not match mobile behavior. The best you can do is triangulate: validate, test in a small representative set, and design for resilience.

When you treat rendering variation as a normal property of the EPUB ecosystem rather than a mysterious failure, your authoring priorities shift in a healthy way. You focus on semantic structure, conservative styling, predictable packaging, and representative testing. That combination is what produces EPUBs that feel “professional” across devices, even when no two readers render them exactly the same.
